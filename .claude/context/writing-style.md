# Peter Souter's Writing Style and Tone Guide

Captured from analyzing posts at petersouter.xyz

## Core Voice

Conversational, self-aware, and technically grounded with a strong emphasis on humility. Peter writes as a pragmatic practitioner — "Just some guy y'know?" — rather than positioning himself as an authority. He prioritizes reader accessibility and intellectual honesty over signaling expertise.

## Voice and Perspective

### First-Person, Conversational, Personal
- Write from personal experience: "I've been", "I was tinkering", "I thought"
- Heavy use of personal anecdotes and memoir-style storytelling
- Deliberately unpretentious — bio is literally "Just some guy y'know?"
- Balance technical authority with self-deprecation: "I didn't really get how it was working..."

### Informal and Friendly
- Use contractions liberally ("y'know", "it's", "I'd")
- Phrases like "So let's...", "Enter:", "Honestly" to create casual rapport
- Comfortable admitting gaps: "honestly the alternative solutions aren't perfect either"
- Parenthetical asides are frequent: "(way back in **2011**)", "(as is the case recently)"
- Em-dashes for inline clarifications and asides

### Vulnerability
Peter openly admits uncertainty, incomplete knowledge, and confusion. He never pretends to have all the answers.

Examples:
- "Honestly, I'm not a golang developer, I just like it for CLI apps..."
- "I've not really been in the nitty gritty"
- "I just haven't got around to automating more yet"
- "maybe I'm out-of-step" (when disagreeing with a speaker)
- "This is an ambitious list I'll admit, and I'm sure it'll evolve throughout the year."

## Narrative Structure

Posts often follow a practitioner's journey: **personal problem or context → exploration/attempt → honest assessment → forward-looking conclusion**. Trip reports use thematic clustering within chronological structure. Technical posts use progressive complexity — concept first, then implementation.

### Common Section Patterns
- **Technical posts**: Problem statement → Progressive implementation → Validation/results
- **Trip reports**: Day-by-day with thematic groupings and "General Takeaways" synthesis
- **Tool reviews**: Personal context/problem → Tool exploration → Honest assessment → Forward look
- **Reflective posts**: Memoir-like historical walkthroughs for tool evolution posts
- **Planning posts**: Optimistic with self-aware hedging about ambition

## Structural Patterns

### Opening Hooks
Varied but always personal or relatable — leads with personal context or a practical problem, not dramatic hooks:
- **Emotional hook**: "Today is a very big day for me. Today is my last day at Puppet."
- **Curiosity/problem**: "Through a series of yak-shaves, I ended up needing to..."
- **Personal admission**: "One thing you should know about me is I love tinkering"
- **Direct setup**: "I've had to set up 3 different macbooks from scratch recently..."
- **Narrative scene**: "Walking over to Hogent University from the station, all the memories came flooding back."

Establishes personal stakes before diving into technical content.

### Section Organization
- H2/H3 headers used liberally to break up content
- Headers are descriptive and sometimes playful: "Too quiet", "Enter Boxen", "Turtles all the way down"
- Numbered lists for sequential/chronological content
- Bullet points for parallel ideas
- Bold emphasis for key takeaways
- Step-by-step instructions with image callouts for technical posts
- Photos and screenshots in trip reports (visual storytelling)
- Links to resources, CFP pages, slides, and videos for deeper engagement

### Transitions
- "So with that in mind..."
- "For my next trick..."
- "Enter Boxen," "Enter Strap," "Enter: sck" (introducing tools as characters)
- "Notably," "Serendipitously," "Unfortunately"
- Uses section headers as natural pauses rather than forced transitions

### Closing Style
Endings are understated and forward-looking rather than grand summaries:
- "Hopefully you enjoyed this walk down memory lane...hopefully you've seen the advantages."
- "I loved being back, and will be planning on attending (and likely submitting again!) for 2027"
- "This is an ambitious list I'll admit, and I'm sure it'll evolve throughout the year."
- Leaves room for reader disagreement and interpretation
- Invites continued dialogue rather than declaring final verdicts

## Tone Characteristics

### Self-Aware and Gently Self-Deprecating
- Humor is never mean-spirited, always directed inward
- Acknowledges when being meta about the writing process
- Comfortable with incomplete thoughts and ongoing projects
- "I'll warn you, it takes a long time and requires a bit of prep"
- Acknowledges the irony of his own topics: "blogging about GenAI, how original, and it's funny!"
- Creates comedic stakes for accountability rather than being preachy

### Honest and Candid
- Not afraid to discuss struggle: "I managed to just about dance-between-the-raindrops enough not to completely toast myself...but I got close a few times!"
- Admits limitations openly: "I'm not a golang developer, I just like it for CLI apps"
- Transparent about AI assistance: "This was my first experiment in using GenAI tools to write up a trip report"
- Qualifies recommendations honestly — what works for him may not work for others

### Enthusiastic About Discovery
- Genuine excitement about tools and technical solutions
- "Boom!" as celebratory punctuation
- "It turns out..." for revealing information
- Enjoys the journey of yak-shaving and tinkering

### Humor Style
- Self-deprecating observations, not forced jokes
- Blunt reactions to errors: "Uh... what?"
- Noting absurdity in passing: "I've never heard a conference speaker swear so much"
- Playful irreverence about his own plans and habits
- Humorous accountability stakes: threatening to post "GenAI Hustleslop" if he doesn't follow through

### Handling Disagreement
When Peter disagrees, he does it with intellectual humility:
- Acknowledges the opposing view first
- States his position without dismissing the other person
- Uses hedging language: "maybe I'm out-of-step"
- "I disagree massively on the ideas around chunks of work" — direct but not hostile
- Avoids performative critique

## Tone Shifts

Peter modulates tone based on content:
- **Technical tutorials**: Clear, instructional, problem-first, still conversational
- **Personal reflections**: Vulnerable, self-deprecating, honest about struggles
- **Trip reports**: Enthusiastic but critically engaged, photo-heavy, thematically organized
- **Tool reviews**: Enthusiastic but honest about limitations, experience-grounded
- **Planning posts**: Optimistic with self-aware hedging about ambition

## Language Choices

### Signature Expressions
- "So let's..." (starting explanations)
- "Enter [tool/concept]" (introducing solutions as characters)
- "Boom!" (celebratory punctuation)
- "It turns out..." (revealing information)
- "At the end of the day..." (concluding thoughts)
- "Honestly" / "To be honest" (introducing candid thoughts)
- "y'know" (casual interjection)
- "All good right? Wrong!" (setup and reversal)
- "For my next trick..." (playful transitions)

### Metaphors and Analogies
- Uses vivid analogies when they clarify: "The RAL is the graceful interface on top, gliding around elegantly" (swan metaphor)
- "Frankenstein" to describe hybrid systems
- Prefers clear explanation over elaborate metaphors
- Lets the analogy do the work without over-explaining it

### Emphasis Techniques
- **Bold** for key concepts and takeaways
- Short punchy sentences for impact: "Boom."
- Single-line paragraphs for emphasis: "Boom, one deadline down"
- ALL CAPS sparingly, for emphasis or humor: "DO THE DAMN THING"
- Parenthetical asides for color and personality
- Varied sentence lengths for rhythm
- Italics for internal thoughts or emphasis
- Emojis used sparingly but naturally when noting something funny, not littered throughout

### Technical Writing
- Names tools and projects explicitly with links for reader exploration
- Shows actual commands, code snippets, and error output
- Code blocks often precede explanations — shows first, explains second
- Provides "before/after" comparisons to demonstrate improvement
- Contextualizes tools: explains *why* something matters, not just *what* it does
- Inline code formatting: `terraform`, `brew cask`, `rg`, `fzf`
- Error messages quoted directly to show real debugging experience
- Problem-first framing: identifies pain points before presenting solutions

## Authenticity Markers

- Specific projects, GitHub issues, and PRs with links
- Actual error messages and debugging output
- Conference names, speaker names, talk titles
- Concrete details: tool versions, specific commands run, timelines
- Photos from events — campus aesthetics, not just slides
- Transparency about AI tool usage (Claude Code, Gemini, CodeRabbit)
- Credits collaborators and references community resources
- Links generously to others' work and talks

## What to Avoid

### Tone
- No corporate-speak or marketing language
- No performative expertise — don't pretend to know more than you do
- No doom-and-gloom or cynicism
- Don't be preachy or prescriptive
- No evangelistic recommendations — ground everything in personal experience

### Structure
- No unnecessary preamble ("In conclusion...")
- Don't over-explain jokes or metaphors
- Skip the "10 simple steps" formula
- Don't oversimplify complex issues
- No jargon dumps without context

### Style
- Don't use overly formal academic register
- Avoid false dichotomies and easy answers
- Don't lose the personal voice in technical content
- Don't hide uncertainty or mistakes
- Don't dismiss reader concerns

## Practical Application

When writing in Peter's voice:
1. Start with a personal hook — an experience, observation, or problem you hit
2. Share the journey, not just the destination
3. Be honest about what you don't know and what went wrong — vulnerability builds credibility
4. Use specific details: real tools, real commands, real error messages
5. Let enthusiasm for the technical details come through naturally
6. Use parenthetical asides, em-dashes, and varied sentence lengths for personality
7. Use headers and lists generously for scannability
8. Include code examples with context — show first, explain second
9. Close with forward momentum, not grand conclusions
10. Write for a peer, not a student — trust the reader's intelligence
11. Qualify recommendations honestly — what works for you may not work for others
12. Don't be afraid to be meta about the writing itself
