Saw a fun little Slack thread this week where someone floated the term "meat tokens" as a way to describe human thinking, riffing off "meat shield" (humans as the accountability layer for AI decisions).

I like it. It lines up neatly with a whole lineage of "meat" metaphors that've been floating around tech culture for decades.

## Meatspace

"[Meatspace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meatspace)" is the old one. Early 90s cyberpunk-adjacent coinage: the world where your body lives, as opposed to cyberspace. The OED picked it up in 2000. It's the grandparent of all of these.

## Meat Tokens

If LLMs tokenise text into chunks, then maybe our squishy biological equivalent is "meat tokens". A unit of thought processed by the organic compute.

It's the kind of term that's half joke, half genuinely useful shorthand when you're writing docs and need to split "this is for the model" from "this is for the h00manz". Romain in the thread called the serious-face version of this "biological attention", which is more technically proper (and way less fun).

## Meat Shield

A meat shield is the human you put in the loop so there's someone accountable when the AI does something weird or wrong. Less about processing and more about liability. Sometimes affectionately called "human-in-the-loop", which sounds much kinder in a compliance review.

## Wetware

The closest respectable cousin. [Wetware](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wetware_(brain)) treats the brain as hardware (neurons) running software (patterns of impulses), with "wet" gesturing at the fact that it's all sloshing around in water. It's been in circulation since well before any of the current AI stuff.

## They're Made Out of Meat

The ur-text. Terry Bisson's 1991 short story ["They're Made Out of Meat"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/They%27re_Made_Out_of_Meat), where two aliens investigating Earth simply cannot believe that thinking, talking, sentient beings are made of *meat*.

> "They're made out of meat."
>
> "Meat?"
>
> "Meat. They're made out of meat."

It's a short read, and it gets funnier the further you get into our current AI moment.

## So What

All of these lean on the same underlying joke: treating humans as a substrate (meat, wet circuitry, a shield, a token stream) is absurd, and also kind of useful as a framing device.

The more you work alongside models, the more the vocabulary stops feeling dystopian and starts feeling like a genuinely handy way to label which bits of a workflow are yours and which bits are the machine's.
