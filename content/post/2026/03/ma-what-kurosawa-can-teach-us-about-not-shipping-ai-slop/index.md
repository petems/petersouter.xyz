OK. Cards on the table. I'm about to use a Japanese aesthetic concept to talk about AI workflows. I *know*. I can already feel the collective eye-roll. A western white guy invoking Eastern philosophy to make a point about technology — how very TED Talk of me.

But hear me out. My intentions are good, and honestly? This is also a rare chance for me to flex my Film Studies A-level, which I haven't really done in earnest since my exams over two decades ago. So indulge me.

## What is Ma (間)?

Ma (間) is a Japanese concept that roughly translates to "the space between things" — but that doesn't really capture it. It's not just *empty* space. It's *intentional* negative space. The gap that gives the things around it their meaning.

In architecture, it's the bare wall that makes the single hanging scroll feel significant. In music, it's the pause between notes that gives the melody its shape. In film, it's the held shot of an empty room that makes you *feel* something before the characters even arrive.

Hayao Miyazaki — yes, the Studio Ghibli guy — explained it perfectly to Roger Ebert in a 2002 interview. He clapped his hands several times and said: *"The time in between my clapping is ma. If you just have non-stop action with no breathing space at all, it's just busyness. But if you take a moment, then the tension building in the film can grow into a wider dimension."*

That's the whole thing. Without the space, everything is just... noise.

## The Rain on the Village

I'll be honest about what re-sparked this for me. I was watching a YouTube clip from a Variety interview with Brennan Lee Mulligan and Matt Mercer — yes, the Critical Role guys — and Mulligan started talking about Kurosawa. Specifically about *Seven Samurai* and the concept of Ma.

Now, before anyone comes for me: I *have* heard of this concept before, thank you very much. We watched Kurosawa in sixth form. I wrote essays about this. But Mulligan articulated something that lodged in my brain and wouldn't leave.

He described the sequence before the final battle in *Seven Samurai*. The action has been building and building throughout the film — bandits, training, tension — and then it just... stops. Kurosawa holds on the village. Rain falling. People waiting. Quiet.

Mulligan's line was: *"If you don't let the audience watch the rain fall on the peaceful village, how will they know what violence is?"*

That's Ma. The pause isn't filler. The pause is the *point*. Without that quiet — without seeing what these people are fighting *for* — the final battle is just choreography. Sound and fury, signifying nothing (sorry, wrong reference — I did English Literature A-level too, I'm on a roll).

Mercer added that he'd had to genuinely fight to protect moments like this in the *Legend of Vox Machina* animated adaptation. In animation, every second costs money, and the instinct is always to cut the "dead air." But it's not dead. It's where the meaning lives.

## Ma Is Everywhere (Once You See It)

This isn't just a film thing. Once you start looking for Ma, it's everywhere.

**In the Totoro bus stop scene**, Satsuki and Mei are just... waiting. In the rain. At night. Mei falls asleep on her sister's back. There's no dialogue, no plot advancement — just rain, frogs, rustling leaves. And then Totoro appears beside them with a leaf on his head. It's one of the most beloved scenes in animation history, and nothing *happens* in it. The Ma *is* the scene.

**Miles Davis** built his entire trumpet style on the notes he *didn't* play. He once said, *"In music, silence is more important than sound."* When Miles paused, the band kept playing around him — his silence wasn't absence, it was a deliberate compositional choice. The space he left was as intentional as the notes he played.

**The Ryoan-ji rock garden in Kyoto** has fifteen stones arranged in raked white gravel. Here's the thing: you can never see all fifteen stones from any single vantage point. The garden is literally, architecturally, about what you *cannot* perceive. The negative space isn't decoration — it's the entire philosophy.

Different art forms. Same principle. The space between things is where the meaning lives.

## So What Does This Have to Do With AI?

Right. The bit you probably came here for.

We're in the middle of an interesting moment with generative AI. There are broadly two camps: the people who've gone all-in on AI-generated everything, and the people who want to reject it entirely. And the conversation between them is — to put it diplomatically — not always productive.

On one side, you've got the "vibe coding" approach applied to everything. Need a blog post? Generate it. Need documentation? Generate it. Need a marketing page? Generate it. Ship it. Move on. The output becomes the product, and the human becomes the person who presses the button.

The result, predictably, is what people have started calling "AI slop." Content that's technically correct but spiritually empty. It reads like it was generated because it *was* generated. There's no Ma. No space for the human voice. No rain on the village before the battle.

On the other side, you've got people who see all of this and conclude that AI tools are inherently the problem. That the only way to avoid slop is to reject the tools entirely. And I get it — the slop is real, and it's everywhere.

But I think both camps are missing something. The problem isn't the AI. The problem is the missing Ma.

## Ma for Agentic Flows

Here's what I mean in practice. When I work with AI tools — and I do, regularly — the value isn't in the output itself. It's in the *space between* the output and what I do with it.

Take this blog, for example. I use Claude as an editor's voice. I'll write something, bounce it off Claude, get suggestions back. But — and this is the crucial bit — I'm the one writing. The thinking happens in the gap. The space between "here's what the AI suggested" and "here's what I actually want to say" is where the real work lives. That pause is my Ma.

It's the same principle applied more broadly:

**Human review points in agentic workflows aren't bottlenecks — they're the point.** When you build an AI pipeline that runs end-to-end with no human pause, you're cutting the rain scene. You're going straight from "bandits are coming" to the battle. And your output will feel exactly as hollow as that sounds.

**Sitting with AI output before prompting again matters.** There's a real temptation to immediately feed output back in, iterate faster, go faster, *more*. But the digestion time — the moment where you actually *think* about what you're looking at — that's where the quality lives. That's where you catch the thing that's technically correct but tonally wrong. That's where *you* show up in the work.

**Thinking out loud is writing.** This is something I genuinely believe. The act of wrestling with an idea, of figuring out what you actually think by trying to articulate it — that's not a step you can skip. When you outsource the writing entirely, you outsource the thinking. And the thinking is the whole point.

**Editing AI output is where it becomes yours.** The raw output is the raked gravel. Your edits, your voice, your decisions about what stays and what goes — that's the stone arrangement. That's what makes it a garden instead of just... gravel.

## The Rain Before Your Battle

Look — I'm not anti-AI. I literally used AI tools while writing this post. I'd be a massive hypocrite if I pretended otherwise.

But I think the conversation about "AI slop" versus "no AI" is missing a third option: AI with Ma. AI with intentional space. AI where the human isn't just pressing the button, but is present in the gaps — thinking, editing, deciding, pausing.

Kurosawa didn't film the rain scene because he ran out of battle footage. He filmed it because he understood that the pause is what makes the payoff land. The space between things is where the meaning lives.

So the next time you're working with an AI tool and you feel that pull to just ship the output — to skip the editing, skip the thinking, go straight to the battle — maybe take a beat. Watch the rain fall on the village for a minute.

You'll know what the violence is when you get there.
