## June 8th 1991 -- February 6th 2026

On February 6th 2026, Terrance Gore passed away following complications from a routine surgery. He was 34 years old. He is survived by his wife, Britney, and their three young children.

It's always tragic when the world loses a husband and father, but Gore's passing was particularly felt in the baseball world. Gore was a Kansas City Royals legend, part of their 2015 World Series-winning small-ball scrappy squad, as well as a runner-for-hire for several teams as a reserve postseason weapon -- winning three rings with the 2015 Royals, 2020 Dodgers, and 2021 Braves.

<!--more-->

Now, the fact that I'm even aware of this news and chose to write about it at all probably won't surprise you: I'm a baseball fan. Whilst baseball isn't as big as it was historically, there are roughly 500 million fans worldwide -- I'm hardly an anomaly. But I'm not from the Americas, where most baseball fans live. I'm British, and the UK is not a baseball country -- in fact, the biggest growing sport here is basketball.

It's a long story how I ended up as a baseball fan, but one of the key reasons it interests me is exemplified by a player like Gore himself: the sheer diversity in roles and abilities that players can have. I'm also a Dodgers fan, which gives me a small personal connection to Gore's story -- more on that later.

But first, let's talk about the core mechanic of baseball.

## The Core Mechanic: 1v1

Baseball is a bit unique in team sport terms. Despite the fact there are between 10 and 13 players on the field at any time (eight fielders, one pitcher, one batter, and between zero and three baserunners), the primary mechanic is a 1v1.

**Batter vs Pitcher.**

A psychological and physical battle between two players. Every pitch is a culmination of incredible skill and athleticism -- throwing a ball at unimaginable speeds (Major Leaguers frequently top 90+ mph, with some elite hurlers reaching 100+ mph), combined with the sneakiness of off-speed pitches that suddenly drop, slide, or cut away from the batter's swing.

Sure, once the ball's in play, the rest of the team expands this original 1v1. You've got fielders looking to catch a ball before it lands, or throw it to a base to get the runner out.

But the main reason Gore was so notable was that he was the type of player who fundamentally broke this core mano-a-mano battle.

## Snagging a Bag

Baserunners generally have a simple role: when the batter puts the ball in play, run to the next base. But there's an extra sneaky mechanic, one that was originally a bending of the rules: nothing in the rulebook said you had to wait until the ball was hit before you start running. And thus, stealing a base was born.

A good base stealer throws a huge wrench into that psychological one-on-one. They're a wildcard. They force the game to move on their terms. The pitcher has to change his whole approach, just because of that one bloke slowly extending his lead away from the bag. He might have to pitch from the stretch. The catcher thinks twice about calling for something the hitter could drive, because they're terrified of the runner getting a jump. It's a whole defensive operating system running in the background the instant you take your lead.

A great pinch-runner doesn't just steal bases -- he steals *certainty*. He's essentially forcing everybody to play the inning twice: once against the batter, and once against the possibility that the runner will simply decide the game on his own.

As former Royals GM Dayton Moore put it: *"There have been very few players who can take over a baseball game. That's exactly what he did."*

And Eric Hosmer, Gore's teammate on those Royals teams, captured it perfectly: *"He was fearless. There's stealing a base, and then there's stealing a base when everyone knows you're trying to steal."*

Gore's legacy, to me, is that he was one of that rare class of player who made the sport a little bit more chaotic and lively.

## The 5-Tool Framework

For those of you who don't know baseball that well, let me explain why Gore was such a special player.

Scouting in baseball is as much an art as it is a science. It uses wonderfully colourful terminology -- "eyewash" for flashy but empty plays, "plus-plus" for elite tools, "soft hands" for a fielder with a smooth glove. One of the key scouting frameworks for position players (i.e. non-pitchers) is the **five tools**:

1. **Contact** -- hitting for average
2. **Power** -- hitting for distance
3. **Speed** -- running ability
4. **Fielding** -- defensive ability in the field
5. **Arm** -- throwing power and accuracy

This is one of the reasons I love baseball. There are so many different kinds of player. You've got monster-power hitters who launch the ball into orbit. Defensive specialists who steal home runs by scaling the outfield wall. Slap-happy contact hitters who might not knock it out of the park but they put the ball in play and help get their teammates home.

A "five-tool player" is the holy grail -- someone who can do it all. Think Mike Trout. Think Mookie Betts.

Gore was not a five-tool player. Or a four-tool player. Or even a three-tool player.

If anything... he was a **one-tool player**.

Speed. Pure, terrifying speed.

But that one tool? That was enough for a Major League career spanning nine seasons and three World Series rings. And this is in a sport where even Hall of Famers miss out on rings.

## 8 Years in the Bigs

From the time he was drafted by the Royals in the 20th round of the 2011 draft out of Gulf Coast State College, Gore never looked like the typical baseball player. He was 5'7" (always among the shortest players in the league), and at 160lbs he was one of the lightest players in the game. But his smaller, leaner frame, combined with incredible baseball IQ when it came to leads and timing, made him a demon on the basepaths.

He came out of the blocks strong. In the **Arizona League (rookie ball)** he hit **.340/.447/.404** across **35 games**, and was already a menace on the bases -- stealing **17 bags** while being caught **zero** times.

In **2012**, he moved up to **Burlington (Appalachian League)** and the bat cooled (.256/.379/.291), but the calling card was obvious: he stole **36 bases** (caught just **twice**) in **61 games**, while drawing **36 walks**. Even as the hitting adjusted with tougher competition, he kept finding ways to reach base and immediately pressure defences.

In **2013** with **Lexington (A-ball)**, he took things to another level: **68 stolen bases** (just **8** caught stealing) in **128 games**. That's an enormous total even in the minors, and it clearly marked him as a pure speed weapon. By the end of his third professional season, he'd stolen **121 bases** in **224 games**.

In **2014**, after spending most of the year at **Wilmington (A+)** where he ranked fourth in the Carolina League with 36 steals, the **Royals called him up to the majors**. He appeared in **11 MLB games** and stole **5 bases** despite getting just **2 plate appearances** -- used almost purely as a late-game pinch-runner. That specialised role carried straight into Kansas City's **2014 postseason run**, where his speed was deployed as a tactical edge even without him needing to hit.

Whilst the Royals ultimately fell to the demon that was postseason Madison Bumgarner, they got a second bite of the apple in 2015, and Gore won his first ring.

From there, he built an MLB career spanning nine seasons primarily as a **pinch runner** -- a postseason weapon for multiple championship teams. He was frequently picked up at the trade deadline by contending teams who wanted a speedster in their back pocket. His career numbers tell the story perfectly: **112 games**, just **14 starts**, **85 plate appearances**, **48 stolen bases** in **58 attempts**. He didn't get his first MLB hit until 2018, his fifth season in the bigs.

## Three Rings, Barely Any At-Bats

This is the part that I find truly remarkable. Gore won two additional World Series titles with the 2020 Dodgers and 2021 Braves, on top of his 2015 Royals ring.

And he barely played for either team!

For the Dodgers in 2020, he played a grand total of **2 regular season games** and **zero playoff games**, despite being on the 28-man roster for the Wild Card and Division Series. As a Dodgers fan, I'd been following Gore's career since his Royals days, so when he briefly wore the blue it felt like a lovely bit of serendipity -- even if he was a Dodger mostly by technicality. For the Braves in 2021, he made **0 regular season appearances** and just **1 playoff appearance** -- as a pinch runner, naturally.

There's a lovely story from the 2021 World Series celebration: Gore FaceTimed injured Braves pitcher Charlie Morton -- who'd broken his fibula in Game 1 -- so Morton could celebrate the title with the rest of the team. That's the kind of teammate Gore was across every clubhouse he entered.

## One Tool Was Enough

Royals President J.J. Picollo said it best: *"You could just feel the energy in the stadium go up a level because of that."*

That's what Gore did. He didn't need five tools. He didn't even need two. He had speed -- devastating, game-altering, certainty-stealing speed -- and he rode that one tool to three championship rings and a nine-season career at the highest level of professional baseball.

In a sport obsessed with five-tool players, with launch angles and exit velocities and all the analytics you can stomach, Gore was a beautiful reminder that sometimes one thing done at an elite level is enough. That there's a place in the game for the specialist, the wildcard, the bloke who changes the entire complexion of an inning just by stepping onto the basepath.

After his playing days, Gore returned to his home in Panama City, Florida, where he worked as a speed and baseball trainer whilst coaching his son's team. He was giving back to the game that gave him so much.

## A Small Tribute

I'm not going to pretend this is some grand obituary or a definitive account of Gore's career. The writers linked above have done that far better than I ever could. This is just a small tribute from a British Dodgers fan who happened to fall in love with the kind of player that Gore represented -- the specialist, the wildcard, the guy who did one thing so well that the entire sport had to make room for him.

I've got his 2020 Dodgers baseball card sitting on my desk as I write this. It's nothing fancy -- a basic Topps card from a season where he barely played. But it's a reminder of that brief window where Gore was technically one of ours, and of the beautiful absurdity that a bloke with 2 regular season games for the Dodgers ended up with a ring.
