I watched [a video essay by Modern MBA](https://youtu.be/evArab8oOoo?si=2DoDzEA_qpMhFs4I) the other day about how the *Moneyball* mindset — not just the book or the film, but the whole philosophy — escaped baseball and infected corporate America. Their argument is that what *The Social Network* was for nerds, *Moneyball* was for suits. The film spread through business schools and boardrooms, and the message that landed wasn't "find market inefficiencies" — it was "winning is a maths problem, not a creative one."

{{< youtube evArab8oOoo >}}

The bit that properly got me was the consumer goods angle. Companies that spent decades actually innovating — new brands, new products, new formats — gradually swapped all that for what Modern MBA calls "the clinical science of value extraction." Cost-cutting isn't new, but Moneyball gave it a heroic narrative. You're not cheapening the product — you're being *smart*. You're finding inefficiencies. You're playing the game better than everyone else.

And it made me think about my own complicated relationship with the film. Because I love *Moneyball*. I genuinely think it's one of the best baseball movies ever made — and probably one of the best sports films full stop. Brad Pitt is magnetic, Jonah Hill is understated and brilliant, and the whole thing *feels* like baseball in a way that most sports movies don't even attempt.

<!--more-->

But talking about it with other baseball fans? That's where it gets tricky. Because you can't separate the movie from what its ideas became. And what its ideas became is... well, let's start with the good bit.

## That Home Run

If you haven't seen it — or if it's been a while — let me set the stage. It's September 4th, 2002. The Oakland A's have won 19 games in a row and they're trying to tie the American League record with number 20. They're up 11-0 against the Kansas City Royals. It should be a comfortable cruise to the finish line.

And then the Royals come back. All the way back. 11-11.

The entire stadium is in that horrible, sinking, "oh no, we've bottled it" mode. And then Scott Hatteberg — converted catcher, poster child for the Moneyball philosophy, the bloke Billy Beane picked up specifically because he had a good on-base percentage — steps up as a pinch hitter and [launches a walk-off home run over the right-centre field wall](https://sabr.org/gamesproj/game/september-4-2002-moneyball-oakland-as-win-20th-game-in-a-row-on-scott-hattebergs-walk-off-homer/).

In the film, [Chris Pratt plays Hatteberg](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt1210166/characters/nm0695435/) — and this is pre-*Guardians of the Galaxy*, pre-*Jurassic World*, pre-overexposure Chris Pratt. He's just this slightly doughy, earnest, likeable guy who can't believe he's actually doing this. He [absolutely nails Hatteberg's real reaction](https://www.mlb.com/cut4/scott-hattebergs-moneyball-hr-extends-streak/c-147158164) — the arm raised, the look back towards his teammates gathering at home plate — and honestly, every time I watch it, I still get chills. If you watch the real footage side by side with the movie, it's uncanny. Hatteberg himself came to the set to help Pratt with the role, and it shows.

It's cinema at its best. It's sport at its best. It's the reason we watch.

So what's the problem?

## The Bit They Left Out

Here's where it gets complicated. The movie tells you the A's won because Billy Beane and his stats guy Paul DePodesta (turned into the fictional "Peter Brand" and played by Jonah Hill) figured out that on-base percentage was undervalued by the market. They picked up cheap players that other teams had overlooked, built a roster on the cheap, and won anyway. David vs Goliath. Brain vs money.

It's a great narrative. It's also... not the whole picture.

The 2002 Oakland A's had what became known as [the "Big Three"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Three_(Oakland_Athletics%29): Barry Zito, Tim Hudson, and Mark Mulder. Zito won the AL Cy Young Award that year with a 23-5 record and a 2.75 ERA. Hudson and Mulder had both finished as Cy Young runners-up in previous seasons. That's not a rotation you assemble by finding undervalued players on the cheap — that's three of the best pitchers in the American League, all on your team at the same time.

[Miguel Tejada won the 2002 AL MVP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2002_American_League_Most_Valuable_Player_Award). He and Eric Chavez combined for 240 RBIs. And here's the irony: Tejada was famously *not* a Moneyball-type player. He swung at everything. [Billy Beane himself called him "Mr. Swing-At-Everything"](https://leejudge.substack.com/p/the-problem-with-moneyball) because he didn't walk enough. But he could absolutely rake.

The movie doesn't really want to tell you about any of that. It wants to tell you about the philosophy. And as a piece of storytelling, that's the right call — the philosophy is what makes it interesting as a *film*. But it means the movie accidentally became something more than just a film. It became a manifesto.

## The Wrong Lesson

The real insight behind Moneyball — the actual clever bit — [wasn't really about advanced statistics at all](https://www.forkingpaths.co/p/the-perils-of-moneyballing-everything). It was about market inefficiency. Billy Beane noticed that the rest of baseball was systematically undervaluing certain player traits (specifically on-base percentage), so he exploited that gap. It's behavioural economics applied to baseball, and it was genuinely brilliant.

But that's not the lesson baseball took away from it.

What the rest of the sport heard was: "Stats beat scouts. Spreadsheets beat intuition. If you can quantify it, you can optimise it." Teams started hiring physics PhDs and building entire analytics departments. They started looking for [new statistics that better correlated to "win probability"](https://iansbnr.com/how-moneyball-ruined-baseball/) than simple metrics like OBP. And eventually, they started optimising the fun right out of the game.

As [former Phillies GM Ruben Amaro put it](https://www.sportskeeda.com/baseball/news-ruined-sport-20-years-ex-phillies-gm-calls-major-flaw-brad-pitt-s-moneyball): the analytics "took baseball backwards... to such an extent that now they're making rules to try to get the game back to where it used to be." He claimed the Moneyball philosophy "ruined the game for like 20 years."

That's probably a bit strong. But he's not entirely wrong, either.

## The Three True Outcomes

If you're not familiar with the term, the ["Three True Outcomes"](https://sabr.org/journal/article/the-growth-of-three-true-outcomes-from-usenet-joke-to-baseball-flashpoint/) refers to the three things that can happen in a plate appearance that don't involve a fielder: a walk, a strikeout, or a home run. The term originally came from a [Usenet joke about Rob Deer](https://ourgame.mlblogs.com/the-three-true-outcomes-and-a-possible-fourth-e1061a2ae445), a player so all-or-nothing that he either walked, struck out, or hit it over the fence.

Analytics-driven baseball took that joke and turned it into a strategy. If you optimise purely for run production, it turns out you want hitters who swing hard (more home runs), take pitches (more walks), and don't worry about contact (more strikeouts). The ball is in play less. [Errors, bunts, stolen bases, hit-and-run plays — all the things that traditionally made baseball exciting — started to disappear](https://chroniclet.com/news/239403/true-outcome-analytics-making-for-boring-games-this-postseason/).

It's not quite "enshittification" — that term implies someone is deliberately extracting value to line their own pockets. This is more like... flattening. Everyone ran the same models, arrived at the same conclusions, and built rosters using the same philosophy. The game got homogenised. Every team was trying to do the same thing, and the thing they were trying to do happened to be quite boring to watch.

## Team Control and the Death of the Star

The other legacy of Moneyball thinking — the one that's perhaps even more damaging to the fan experience — is [what it did to how teams build rosters](https://theconversation.com/baseballs-biggest-problem-isnt-pace-of-play-its-teams-tanking-113094).

If you can find undervalued players cheaply, why bother paying for expensive stars? If your analytics department can identify which minor leaguers are most likely to outperform their salary, why sign a free agent to a massive deal? The logical endpoint of Moneyball thinking isn't just "find value" — it's "avoid paying market rate."

This led directly to the tanking era. Teams like the Astros and Cubs [deliberately gutted their rosters for years](https://www.espn.com/mlb/story/_/id/32008247/the-end-tanking-how-make-every-mlb-team-try-win-every-year), stockpiling cheap young players with [six years of team control before they could reach free agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Service_time_manipulation), knowing they could compete later with a roster full of pre-arbitration players making the league minimum. And it worked — both won World Series. So naturally, everyone else tried it too.

The result? Multiple teams each year who aren't even *trying* to win. Marquee free agents sitting unsigned because teams would rather roll with a cheaper, slightly worse option who they can control for longer. Fans in half a dozen cities knowing their team has no intention of competing this year, or next year, or possibly the year after that.

And then there's my team.

I'm a Dodgers fan, which means I have an... awkward relationship with all of this. Andrew Friedman — the guy who now runs the Dodgers — cut his teeth doing exactly the Moneyball thing with the Tampa Bay Rays. Small market, tiny budget, analytics-driven roster construction. He was *great* at it. And then in 2014, he came to the Dodgers, where the ownership group essentially said "right, do all that clever stuff, but also here's a blank cheque."

The result is the most dominant team in modern baseball. Back-to-back World Series. Shohei Ohtani on a $700 million contract — the largest in sports history. Yoshinobu Yamamoto lured over from Japan on a $325 million deal before he'd thrown a single pitch in the majors. Roki Sasaki persuaded to sign based on the Dodgers' analytics operation, their pitching development lab, their track record. It's Moneyball with money, and it turns out that's an almost unfair combination.

Which puts me in the bizarre position of benefiting directly from the same philosophy I've just spent several paragraphs criticising. The Dodgers are the final evolution of Moneyball thinking — they just happen to also have the resources to buy the stars that Billy Beane could never afford. I can hardly complain about analytics ruining the game while my team is using those same analytics to build a dynasty.

Like I said: it's complicated.

## So Where Does That Leave the Movie?

This is the tension I can never quite resolve, and why it's such a tricky movie to talk about with other baseball fans.

As a film, *Moneyball* is nearly perfect. The pacing is excellent, the performances are brilliant, and it captures something genuinely moving about sport — the idea that there's more than one way to win, that underdogs can find an edge, that sometimes the system needs someone to challenge it.

But as a cultural artefact, it's also the origin story for a set of ideas that, when taken to their logical extreme, have made modern baseball — and quite a lot of consumer capitalism — demonstrably less enjoyable for the average person. The Three True Outcomes. Tanking. Service time manipulation. The slow death of the mid-market star. Your favourite cereal box getting smaller while the price goes up. It's all part of the same mindset: find the inefficiency, exploit it, extract the value, move on.

It's both things at once. And I think that's okay. You can love the movie and hate what its ideas became. You can get chills when Hatteberg rounds the bases and still be frustrated when your team trades away its best player for three 22-year-olds in A-ball. You can admire Billy Beane's ingenuity and still wish everyone else hadn't taken entirely the wrong lesson from it. You can be a Dodgers fan and feel slightly guilty about the whole thing.

It's a complicated movie — not in a "the plot is hard to follow" way, but in a "the real-world consequences of its central thesis turned out to be a bit of a nightmare" way.

But I'll still watch it again. And I'll still get chills when that ball goes over the wall. Every single time.
